INTRODUCTION AND SUMMARY

Topic Introduction

Our topic is focused on wildfires across the United States that took place between 1992 and 2015. U.S. wildfires are important to analyze as they impact people, wildlife, the economy, and communities/ecosystems across the country. They are the cause of extensive harm from spreading air pollution to long-term vegetation damage. Therefore, analyzing historic wildfires could help make informed, preventative decisions about future wildfires. With climate change on the rise, it will also be interesting to see wildfire incidents over time. It's important to consider the locations, causes, dates, sizes, and other patterns of past wildfires to perhaps be able to assess risks, understand causes, and improve prevention strategies. Overall, analyzing wildfires could help with both immediate damage responses to long-term planning for reducing their effects or limiting their occurrences. This information can then be especially useful to policymakers but also the general public as they can be directly or indirectly affected.

WILDFIRE DATA

Introduction to the data

The wildfire dataset we used was compiled from multiple federal, state, and local fire reports. The dataset was downloaded from Kaggle, however the original dataset format was a sqlite database, so it was necessary to convert the sqlite database to a pandas dataframe, then save the dataframe as a csv file to make the data easier to work with. The dataset consists of 1.88 million geo-referenced wildfire records, and represents 140 million acres of land burned that occurred in the United States from 1992 to 2015. The dataframe initially had 51 columns, and to clean our data, we went through the raw data and selected relevant variables, which included the following features:

Variable Name Details
fire_name Name of the incident, from the fire report (primary) or ICS-209 report (secondary).
fire_year Calendar year in which the fire was discovered or confirmed to exist.
discovery_DOY Day of year on which the fire was discovered or confirmed to exist.
stat_cause_code Code for the (statistical) cause of the fire.
stat_cause_descr Description of the (statistical) cause of the fire.
cont_date Date on which the fire was declared contained or otherwise controlled.
fire_size Estimate of acres within the final perimeter of the fire.
fire_size_class Code for fire size based on the number of acres within the final fire perimeter expenditures
latitude Latitude for point location of the fire (decimal degrees).
longitude Longitude for point location of the fire (decimal degrees).
state Two-letter alphabetic code for the state in which the fire burned (or originated), based on the nominal designation in the fire report.
county County, or equivalent, in which the fire burned (or originated), based on nominal designation in the fire report.
FIPS_code Three-digit code from the Federal Information Process Standards (FIPS) publication 6-4 for representation of counties and equivalent entities.
FIPS_name County name from the FIPS publication 6-4 for representation of counties and equivalent entities.

After cleaning and dropping rows with invalid values, our new dataframe had 14 columns and 1880464 rows. STATIC VISUALIZATIONS

Map of Wildfire Sizes Across the U.S.

Here we present a map of wildfire sizes across the U.S., specifically representing fire sizes by acres within the final perimeter of the fire. A red color map is used to signify smaller fires (lighter red) to larger fires (darker red). We can identify that Alaska has larger fires overall, but the amount doesn't cover the whole state. This is somewhat surprising since Alaska is a relatively cold state simultaneously understandable that the fires are larger perhaps due to less population and vast nature. In general, this map allows us to see that there are smaller fires on the coasts (specifically the west and northeast), and there are medium sized fires more towards the middle of the U.S. This could be due to population reasons and access to coast water/coast climate.

Line Chart of Wildfire Occurrences over the Years

Moreover, we wanted to examine wildfire occurrences over the years (from 1992 to 2015). Since we are utilizing Altair, it's important to note the restriction of having chosen a random 5000 data points as opposed to the whole data set.

Bar Chart of Wildfire Occurrences by U.S. State
